# Oxalic acid–glycerine strips: a validated recipe and dosing formula for Dadant-Blatt hives

**Your recipe works, but it is under-dosed by roughly half, your preparation temperature of 55 °C is on the low-safe side (fine, just slow), and your intuition about thymol promoting chewing is supported by one of Randy Oliver's own pilot tests — with an important queen-loss caveat that dictates a tight upper limit.** The oxalic-acid/glycerine (OA/Gly) contact-strip method is the single most studied homemade Varroa treatment of the last decade, with peer-reviewed efficacies of 87–97 % when correctly dosed (Maggi 2016 *Apidologie*; Kanelis 2023 *J. Apic. Res.*; VarroxSan registration trials 2024). The mechanism is bee-mediated contact transfer of a hygroscopic acid film, not vapor, which is why the method uniquely keeps working across multiple brood cycles and is essentially temperature-independent *as long as bees are mobile on the strips* (i.e. not in winter cluster). For your Dadant-Blatt hives you should scale the validated Langstroth doses up by a factor of ≈1.3 (Dadant combs are 31 % larger by area) and deliver via **four** strips per colony rather than two. Below is the chemistry, physics, and the numerical recipe.

## What the published literature actually says

Three independent, reproducible protocols define the defensible parameter space:

| Protocol | OA:Gly ratio (w/w or w/v) | Total OAD per colony | Strips / colony | Duration | Peer-reviewed efficacy |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Aluen CAP (Maggi 2016, Argentina) | 1 : 2 (v/v) | 40 g (4 × 10 g) | 4 U-shapes over frames 2, 4, 6, 8 | 42 d | 93.1 % |
| Randy Oliver "1:1 OAE" (scientificbeekeeping.com) | 1 : 1 (w/w) | ~50 g (2 Swedish sponges / Maximizer pads) | 2 pads, 355 cm² total | 42–60 d | ~90 % absolute (Henderson-Tilton 97–98 %) |
| Kanelis 2023 (Greece, commercial "Oxistrip" basis) | 60 g OA : 130 mL gly : 100 mL water (21 % w/v) | ~40 g | 4 | 42 d | 90.4–94.5 % (4-yr mean) |
| VarroxSan® (Vita, US EPA 2023) | cellulose strip, 18.42 % OAD | ~28–56 g (7 g/strip) | 4 (single) – 6 (double) | 42–56 d | 96.8 % |

All use **oxalic acid dihydrate** (C₂H₂O₄·2H₂O, MW 126.07), not anhydrous. This matters for dosing — if you weigh out "oxalic acid", double-check your label: the dihydrate is the cheap, standard technical/feed-grade form, and essentially everything in the beekeeping literature is expressed in grams of dihydrate. A published "50 g OA" dose is 50 g of dihydrate, which is 35.7 g of anhydrous OA plus 14.3 g of water of crystallisation.

**Your current recipe (1 teaspoon thymol, two cotton swabs between frames at 1/3 and 2/3 from the entrance) is a roughly 40–50 % dose of Aluen/Oliver per 10-frame Langstroth equivalent, and ≈35 % per Dadant-Blatt colony.** This is the single biggest issue. Greg Rodgers' 2023–24 field data (cited by Oliver) make the dose response explicit: two OA strips in brood-pressed hives gave 13 mites/300; four strips gave 5/300 — an unambiguous dose effect in the range you are currently operating in.

## Why 55 °C is safe but suboptimal, and what actually happens in the pan

Oliver's written protocol specifies **60–71 °C (140–160 °F)** for dissolution, warns that bubbling begins above **77 °C (170 °F)**, and bans prolonged heating above that threshold. The chemistry behind the warning:

- **OA solubility in glycerol is ~500 g/kg**, endothermic, and kinetically slow at 55 °C because glycerol viscosity at 55 °C is still ~100 cP; at 70 °C it drops to ~30 cP, and dissolution time falls from ≈30 min to ≈5 min.
- **Above 77 °C** two unwanted reactions begin: (i) thermal decarboxylation of OA → **HCOOH + CO₂** (that tangy smell = formic acid evolution; losing active ingredient and producing a second miticide whose concentration you cannot control); (ii) **Fischer esterification** of the glycerol primary hydroxyls by OA, giving mono- and di-glyceryl oxalate esters (Alksnis 1976 *J. Polym. Sci.* 14:2631; Kiyotsukuri 1994). Esters are not necessarily bad as slow-release reservoirs, but they are uncharacterised in bee contact studies — avoid them until somebody does the FTIR/HPLC work (none published to date).
- **The "clearing" you observe at ~55 °C is pure dissolution**, not a reaction. At 1:1 OAD:glycerine the mixture becomes visually clear around 57 °C (135 °F) as the dihydrate releases its water of crystallisation (2 × 18 g of H₂O per 126 g OAD = 14.3 wt %, which creates a plasticising glycerol-water co-solvent).
- **99 % anhydrous glycerine is fine; so is 85 % USP.** The Kanelis protocol actually *adds* 100 mL water per 130 mL glycerine precisely because some water improves dispersion into cellulose and strip handling. Your 99 % glycerine plus the water released by the dihydrate already gives ~10–12 % water in the final mix, comparable to Kanelis.

**Recommendation:** prepare at **65–70 °C (150–160 °F)** with a digital thermometer. You will get faster dissolution, zero decomposition, and the mixture clears in ≈3–5 min. Never exceed 75 °C. Use a stainless-steel pan; glycerol + OA etches aluminium.

## The mechanism is contact, not vapor — and that defines the entire protocol

The mite is killed by **protons, not by oxalate**: Nanetti's foundational comparison of OA versus neutral potassium oxalate shows only the acidic form kills Varroa (reviewed in Rademacher & Harz 2006 *Apidologie* 37:98). With pKa₁ = 1.25 and pKa₂ = 4.14, the strip-surface pH is ≈1, so the un-ionised H₂C₂O₄ species (the lipid-permeable form) is abundant, partitions through the thin mite cuticle and empodia (adhesive tarsal pads), dissociates in the more-neutral hemolymph, and the trapped protons + calcium-oxalate precipitation destroy mitochondrial function and Malpighian tubule clearance. **The bee-to-mite selectivity is ≈70-fold on a per-mass basis** (Aliano, Ellis & Siegfried 2006 *J. Econ. Entomol.* 99:1579), driven by three differences: (1) the mite's surface-area-to-volume ratio is ≈10× a worker bee's; (2) the bee's waxy, thick epicuticle repels aqueous acid while mite empodia stay wet and absorbent; (3) bees excrete OA via Malpighian tubules within ~2 h (Nozal 2003), mites cannot.

**Glycerol's role is fourfold and explains why the strip outperforms dribble and vapor:** it is a humectant that pulls hive moisture (55–65 % RH outside brood cells) into the strip, keeping OA in solution as a viscous acidic film rather than recrystallising; it is adhesive enough to stick to bee tarsi and cuticular hairs; it is bitter, so bees don't eat it; and it is essentially non-volatile (BP 290 °C) so it stays on the strip for weeks. **OA vapor pressure at 35 °C hive temperature is ~10⁻⁶ mmHg** — empirically zero — so no airborne dispersal occurs; Ellis & Aliano's 2005 partition experiments showed OA moves colony-wide by **bee-to-bee contact** (trophallaxis is negligible). This is why **the method has no ambient-temperature restriction** (VarroxSan label: "no temperature restriction"): what it needs is bees actively walking over and chewing the strip, which happens whenever the cluster is loose enough for normal in-hive traffic — roughly when daytime ambient exceeds ~10 °C and the cluster is not tight.

The critical implication for a year-round Romanian operation: **the strip handles brood presence that dribble and sublimation cannot**. OA does not penetrate wax cappings (Gregorc & Planinc 2002), so dribble/vapor only kill phoretic mites in a single pulse. A 42–56-day strip spans 2–3 full Varroa reproductive cycles (≈17 d each), catching mites as they emerge from each successive brood cohort. This is the only reason strips work in brood-right colonies at all.

## A validated dosing formula for Dadant-Blatt

The three peer-reviewed protocols cluster tightly when normalised per Langstroth frame of bees:

- Aluen: 40 g OAD / 10 Langstroth frames = **4.0 g/frame**
- Oliver 1:1: 50 g OAD / 20 combs in a double-deep = **2.5 g/frame**
- VarroxSan: 7 g/strip × 1 strip per 2.5 frames of bees = **2.8 g/frame**

**Target range: 2.5–4.0 g OAD per Langstroth frame of bees. Central value ≈ 3 g/frame.**

Converting to Dadant-Blatt: Dadant combs are 435 × 300 mm vs Langstroth deep 448 × 232 mm — comb area per side is **~1,134 cm² vs ~864 cm² = 31 % larger**, and frames host proportionally ~30 % more bees when fully covered. The correct scaling unit is *frames of bees*, and a Dadant frame of bees contains 1.31× the bee mass of a Langstroth frame, so the dose scales by that factor:

$$m_{OAD}\ \text{(grams, dihydrate)} \approx 3.3\,\text{g/frame} \times N_{\text{Dadant frames of bees}}$$

with a practical envelope of 2.8–4.5 g/frame. **For a 10-frame Dadant colony fully covered, this gives 33 g OAD (range 28–45 g).** Use 1:1 w/w glycerine — so 33 g glycerine. Split across **four strips at positions 2, 4, 6, 8** (this is Aluen/VarroxSan standard and reliably outperforms two-strip placements in the field). Your "1/3 and 2/3 from entrance" concept is sound in spirit but should be expanded to four equidistant points.

A ready-reference table for Romanian Dadant conditions:

| Frames of bees (Dadant) | OAD (g) | Glycerine (g, 1:1) | Thymol (g, optional) | Number of strips/swabs | Per-strip OAD (g) |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 4 (small nuc / late-autumn remnant) | 13 | 13 | 1 | 2 | 6.5 |
| 6 (nuc or spring single) | 20 | 20 | 1.5 | 3 | 6.7 |
| 8 (healthy single Dadant) | 26 | 26 | 2 | 4 | 6.5 |
| 10 (full Dadant brood box) | 33 | 33 | 2.5 | 4 | 8.3 |
| 12 (strong, two-storey Dadant) | 40 | 40 | 3 | 4–6 | 6.7–10 |
| 16+ (double Dadant brood chambers) | 53 | 53 | 4 | 6 | 8.8 |

**Temperature window is binary, not graduated.** Above ~10 °C daytime ambient the strips deliver their full dose regardless of temperature, because bees stay active on them and the brood nest is held at 33–36 °C internally year-round. Below that, in a tight winter cluster, strips become inert — do not waste them; use a single OA trickle (3.5 % OAD in 1:1 sugar syrup, 5 mL per occupied bee space, max 50 mL/colony) or sublimation at a broodless moment. **There is no documented damage from strips in Romanian-summer highs of 30–35 °C** (unlike formic acid, which becomes dangerous above 29 °C). So in practice your calendar is: strips April–early October; trickle or sublimation at winter solstice if broodless. One full course per spring after main buildup plus one immediately post-extraction in late summer is the validated two-course annual schedule; more than that risks cumulative sublethal effects (Schneider 2012; Sabahi 2020 flagged OA-glycerin towels as the only treatment increasing bee mortality above controls).

## Your thymol addition is defensible at low dose — and dangerous at high dose

You are in fact reproducing an experiment Randy Oliver published informally in 2017, where beekeeper Chuck Cook tested thymol at 0, 0.25, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, and 2.0 g of thymol per mL of glycerine on shop-towel strips in five hives. The result was exactly what you've observed: **bees avoid plain glycerine strips (bitter, sticky, no stimulus), but chew and redistribute thymol-laced strips in a dose-dependent fashion, with optimal removal at ~1 g thymol per mL glycerine.** However, **two queens died in Cook's five-hive trial at the 1.5–2.0 g/mL levels.** Oliver's specific caution: "If one were to add 12 g of thymol to a shop towel, it would also likely be too much." Apiguard, for reference, delivers 25 g thymol per colony over 4–6 weeks via vapor — so contact-strip thymol doses should sit well below this.

Your "~1 teaspoon" is 2.5–3 g thymol crystals per batch. If that batch makes strips for one colony (4 strips, ~33 g OAD total), you are delivering ~2.5–3 g thymol/colony — roughly **10 % of the Apiguard equivalent dose**, and **0.1–0.2 g thymol/mL glycerine** — four to ten times *below* Cook's optimal chewing concentration but also comfortably below his queen-loss threshold. This is a reasonable, conservative place to be. **Do not push beyond ~5 g thymol per Dadant colony** (roughly 1.5 g/strip); that is the empirical safety envelope.

Two chemistry caveats: (1) Thymol's solubility in glycerol at room temperature is only **~8 g/L (0.8 % w/v)**; at 55–70 °C it increases because thymol melts at 49–52 °C and becomes miscible with warm glycerol, but on cooling most re-precipitates as micro-crystals in the cellulose/cotton matrix — this is *fine* and is essentially how Apiguard works at slow release. (2) There is **no published FTIR/NMR characterisation of OA+thymol+glycerine strip solutions**; thymol's phenolic OH can in principle participate in esterification with OA above ~80 °C, which is another reason to keep preparation below 70 °C.

## Refined recipe and application protocol for your operation

**Preparation (for 4 strips = one 10-frame Dadant colony):**

1. Weigh 33 g oxalic acid dihydrate (confirm "dihydrate" on the label) and 33 g of 99 % glycerine into a stainless-steel pan.
2. Heat to **65–70 °C with a thermometer**, stirring continuously. Do not exceed 75 °C. Dissolution completes in 3–5 min; the mixture clears to a faint yellow-straw colour.
3. Remove from heat. Add **2–2.5 g thymol crystals** (about ¾ teaspoon, not a full teaspoon — weigh if possible; 1 level teaspoon of thymol crystals is typically 2.5–3 g). Stir until dissolved; some thymol may recrystallise as the solution cools below ~50 °C — that is acceptable.
4. Immediately saturate four precut cotton-mop/broom strips of ~2–3 g dry mass each (roughly 20 × 3 cm). Each strip should absorb ~16 g of solution (≈8 g OAD + 8 g glycerine + ~0.6 g thymol). Cotton absorbs 5–8× its own weight; if your strips drip, trim them.
5. Let strips cool and equilibrate in a sealed glass jar for at least an hour before application. They will remain active for months if stored sealed at room temperature.

**Application (Dadant-Blatt brood chamber):**

Place strips **vertically between frames, resting on the top bars and hanging down into the inter-frame space**, at positions 2-4-6-8 (numbering from one side). For an 8-frame occupation, omit position 8; for a 6-frame occupation, use three strips at 2-4-6. Your "1/3 and 2/3 from entrance" geometry is close to correct for two strips but should be expanded to four for full colonies. Leave strips in for **42 days minimum, 56 days maximum**. Do a mite-count (alcohol wash or sugar roll, 300 bees) before and after; expect 85–95 % reduction in a healthy temperate colony, less in autumn high-mite emergencies.

**Year-round Romanian schedule:**

- **Late March / April (post-wintering, buildup):** first course, 42 days. Remove remnants.
- **August (post-extraction):** second course, 56 days. This is the critical treatment that protects winter bees.
- **December (broodless window, daytime >3 °C):** a single OA trickle (3.5 %) if mite counts remain elevated — do not try to use strips in the cluster.
- Rotate with a thymol (Apiguard/Thymovar) or amitraz (Apivar) course once every 2–3 years to pre-empt the (still theoretical) risk of resistance; no OA resistance has been demonstrated in 30 years of intensive use (Kosch 2024, *Insects*; Maggi 2017), but rotation is prudent.

## Safety, legality, and honey issues

**Beekeeper PPE:** OA dihydrate dust is EPA Toxicity Category 1 for eyes. For mixing, wear nitrile gloves, sealed goggles or a face shield, and at minimum an N95 — ideally a P100 half-mask. Heating to 70 °C produces essentially no OA vapor (vapor pressure ~10⁻⁶ mmHg at that temperature) but splatter is corrosive and glycerine will carry it stubbornly into skin. Thymol is a skin/eye irritant; the same gloves and goggles cover it. Oral lethal dose of OA in an adult human is **15–30 g**; store labelled and away from kitchen.

**Honey residues:** Oxalic acid carries a **"no MRL required" status under Commission Regulation (EU) No 37/2010, Table 1** (allowed substance, no withdrawal period on the residue side), because honey already contains 5–300 mg/kg natural oxalate and typical strip treatments do not raise residues detectably above baseline (Maggi 2016; Bartlett 2025). **But:** every EU-authorised OA veterinary medicinal product (Api-Bioxal, Oxuvar, VarroMed, Oxybee, Varroxal) forbids use during nectar flow / with supers on, and your homemade strip inherits that *precautionary* rule. **Do not leave strips in when supers are on or a flow is active.** Also note: OA-glycerine strips measurably raise glycerol content in propolis (Greek study: 20.5 % → 24.3 %) — avoid if you harvest propolis.

**Regulatory status in the EU and Romania:** this is where I have to be blunt. **No OA-glycerine strip formulation currently holds an EU or Romanian marketing authorisation.** VarroxSan is US-only (EPA 2023). Oxybee (Andermatt BioVet, nationally authorised in most MS including Romania) is the one EU OA-glycerol-thymol-anise product — but it is licensed for **trickling only, brood-free**, not as a strip. Under Regulation (EU) 2019/6 Articles 112–114 (the cascade), a veterinarian may prescribe an unauthorised product only if no authorised alternative exists; since Api-Bioxal, Oxuvar, VarroMed and Oxybee all exist for varroosis, self-prepared strips fall outside the cascade. ANSVSA in Romania applies the regulation directly. In practice, Romanian suppliers (apis-blaj.ro, apicris.ro, magazinulapicultorului.ro) openly sell imported Greek "Oxistrip" and DIY strip kits, and enforcement against small-scale sideliner self-preparation is effectively nil — but you are operating in a grey zone, and honey from treated colonies, while chemically unproblematic, is technically "produced from animals treated with an unauthorised VMP." If you sell honey commercially, the cleanest path is to use Oxybee for trickling and time strip use strictly to broodless / no-super periods.

## Where the method genuinely fails, and what not to believe

Three failure modes are real and worth flagging. First, in **hot humid summer climates** (Bartlett 2023 in Georgia, USA; Hristov 2025 in Bulgaria) strips underperform — possibly because high hive humidity saturates the glycerine past its water-equilibrium point and leaches OA into droplets that damage brood instead of delivering acid to bees. Romanian summers tend toward dry continental, which should be fine, but watch for dripping strips and reduce glycerine content (toward 1:0.7) if your strips pool liquid on the bottom board. Second, strips are **not an emergency knock-down** for autumn colonies already at >5 mites/100 bees — they need 2–4 weeks to reach peak activity and cannot catch a collapsing colony; pair such colonies with a single OA sublimation at start of strip treatment. Third, **winter cluster application is futile**: cold bees do not walk on strips. Use a dribble in late December instead.

Claims worth treating skeptically: (a) "Krisztián Hernádi method" — I could not verify any such published or online protocol; if you have a primary source in Hungarian, please share, because it does not appear in English, Hungarian, or German apicultural literature as of this writing. (b) Any recipe that specifies heating above 80 °C — this invites formic acid generation and uncontrolled ester formation. (c) "Strips work at any temperature" in the literal sense — they do not, they work whenever bees move on them, which is a behavioural not a chemical constraint.

## The bottom line

The method is **chemically sound, mechanistically well-understood, and efficacious at 87–97 %** when dosed to published targets. Your current recipe is directionally correct on every axis — right active ingredient, right carrier, right temperature (low end), right placement concept, and a defensible thymol addition that has in fact been piloted by Randy Oliver with favourable chewing response. The three changes that will move your outcome from "probably working" to "reliably matching the Aluen/Oliver benchmark" are: **(1) double the dose to ≈3.3 g OAD per Dadant frame of bees, delivered via four strips not two**; **(2) raise your preparation temperature to 65–70 °C** for faster, cleaner dissolution; and **(3) weigh your thymol to ≤2.5 g per colony batch rather than measuring by teaspoon**, because the Cook/Oliver queen-loss data show a narrow therapeutic window above ~1 g thymol per mL glycerine. Treat strips as your April–October Varroa workhorse, keep one winter OA-dribble in reserve for broodless knock-down, rotate with thymol or amitraz every few years, and you have a year-round programme that matches any EU-authorised product on efficacy — with the regulatory caveat that the strip form remains off-label in Romania, a risk you bear as a sideliner.
