# Expert beekeeping management plan for Baia Mare, Romania

A hobbyist running **4–5 production colonies boosted by 2–3 helper colonies** in Baia Mare can realistically target **15–25 kg of acacia honey and 10–20 kg of linden honey per production hive in a good year**, provided colony populations peak precisely when the notoriously weather-sensitive salcâm and tei flows arrive. The entire management year hinges on one arithmetic fact: **an egg laid today becomes a forager 42 days from now**. Every decision below flows from that number. This plan integrates Buckfast×Carniolan colony dynamics, Maramureș phenology, Romanian beekeeping practices, and Varroa management into a unified week-by-week calendar covering February through October.

## Answering the three key research questions

### 1. Optimal brood nest size: 7 frames is correct — with caveats

The beekeeper's target of **7 frames of brood is well-calibrated for a queen laying ~1,500 eggs/day**. Here is the math. A Dadant deep frame (435 × 300 mm) holds approximately **8,500 total cells**, of which roughly **6,000–6,500 are usable for brood** after accounting for the honey/pollen arc at the top and corners. A queen laying 1,500 eggs/day fills 31,500 cells over the 21-day worker development cycle. At 80% brood coverage per frame (realistic, not wall-to-wall), this requires about **6.5–7 frames**. For an exceptional Buckfast×Carniolan queen laying 1,800 eggs/day, the target rises to **8–9 frames** — which aligns precisely with Brother Adam's own benchmark that a Buckfast queen must fill "at least 8–9 Dadant combs with brood."

The critical nuance is timing. The brood nest should hit maximum size **not during the flow but 6 weeks before it**. For Baia Mare's salcâm peak around May 20–25, peak brood should occur by **April 8–13**. Colony equilibrium population follows the formula: **maximum bees ≈ 42 × daily egg-laying rate**. A queen at 1,500 eggs/day produces an equilibrium colony of ~63,000 bees; boosted with helper colony brood, this can temporarily reach **80,000–90,000 bees**, creating a massive forager surplus during the flow window.

**Recommendation**: Maintain the 7-frame target for average queens. If your queens consistently fill 8+ frames, let them — but ensure this peak occurs by mid-April, not mid-May. During the actual flow, expect and welcome the colony's natural tendency to reduce brood as bees backfill cells with nectar.

### 2. Baia Mare bloom calendar: 10–14 days later than Bucharest

Baia Mare sits at 47.65°N latitude, 228 m elevation, with a continental Carpathian-influenced climate. All bloom dates run **10–14 days behind southern Romania** (sometimes 2–3 weeks in cold springs). The bloom sequence, with typical dates and temperature triggers:

| Species | Bloom start | Peak | Bloom end | Duration | Temperature trigger | Nectar value |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Willow** (Salix spp.) | Mar 15 | Late Mar | Apr 15 | 3–4 weeks | Daytime >10–12°C | Pollen + minor nectar |
| **Fruit trees** (cherry → plum → pear → apple) | Apr 10 | Apr 20–May 1 | May 10 | 3–4 weeks total | Cherry: 15 days >8°C; Apple: sustained 12–15°C | Moderate; maintenance flow |
| **Dandelion** | Apr 10 | Late Apr–mid May | May 20 | 5–6 weeks | Sustained 10°C+ | Moderate nectar + pollen |
| **Horse chestnut** | May 5 | May 10–15 | May 25 | 10–15 days | Mid-spring warmth | Low nectar; good pollen |
| **Black locust / Salcâm** | **May 15–25** | **May 20–Jun 1** | **May 28–Jun 8** | **8–12 days** | **Night >14°C (any secretion), >18°C (good flow); Day >20°C sustained; ~450–500 GDD base 10°C** | **Extremely high** |
| **Catalpa** | Jun 10 | Jun 15–25 | Jul 5 | 2–3 weeks | Needs significant heat accumulation | Moderate–high per tree; limited area |
| **Linden T. platyphyllos** | Jun 15 | Jun 20–25 | Jul 1 | 10–14 days | 16–26°C optimal | High |
| **Linden T. cordata** (MAIN) | **Jun 25** | **Jul 1–8** | **Jul 15** | **12–16 days** | **16–26°C; humidity critical; no secretion <10°C or >35°C** | **Very high** |
| **Goldenrod / late flora** | Aug 1 | Aug 15–Sep 1 | Sep 30 | 6–8 weeks | Summer warmth | Low–moderate; maintenance |

**How to judge season timing**: If cherry blooms before April 10, the season is early; expect salcâm around May 10–15. If cherry hasn't opened by April 20, the season is late; salcâm may not arrive until June 1. Black locust blooms roughly **5 weeks after fruit tree peak**. Linden T. cordata follows salcâm by **20–30 days**. Track cumulative GDD (base 10°C) from January 1 — salcâm triggers at 450–500 GDD.

**Weather sensitivity is extreme.** Salcâm delivers a harvestable crop in roughly **6–7 years out of 10**. Linden produces well only **4–6 years out of 10**. A single cold snap, heavy rain, or windstorm during the 8-day salcâm bloom window can destroy the entire crop.

### 3. Helper colony brood yield: 1 frame per week is the sustainable limit

At any given moment, **57% of all brood is capped** (12 capped days out of the 21-day cycle). A helper colony with 7 frames of brood contains roughly **4 frames' worth of capped brood** at all times. A queen at 1,500 eggs/day produces **~1.75 new Dadant frames of capped brood per week** (10,500 eggs ÷ 6,000 cells/frame).

Sustainable extraction rates break down as follows. **One frame per 7–10 days is fully sustainable** — the queen replaces the cells within 4 days, and the colony barely notices. Two frames per week is marginal: the colony removes more bees than the queen can replace (12,000 cells extracted vs. 10,500 laid), creating a slow decline that collapses the helper within a month. Three frames per week is destructive and unsustainable.

**Practical protocol**: From each helper colony, extract **1 frame of mostly-capped brood every 7–10 days**. Replace it with drawn comb (never foundation — it delays replenishment by a week). With 3 helper colonies, that yields **3 frames of capped brood per week** for distribution across 4–5 production colonies. Begin extractions 5 weeks before the salcâm peak. Over 5 weeks, each production colony receives **3–4 frames of capped brood**, contributing roughly **18,000–24,000 additional bees** that will be forager-age during the main flow.

Never reduce a helper colony below **4 frames of brood** and sufficient nurse bees to cover them (~6,400 bees minimum on those frames). Monitor helper colonies weekly for signs of stress, queenlessness, or disease.

## Week-by-week management plan: February through October

### Late winter assessment and first feeding (Weeks 1–4: February 1 – February 28)

**Week 1–2 (Feb 1–14): Passive monitoring.** Do not open hives. Listen at the entrance with a rubber tube — a steady, even hum indicates a healthy cluster. Check entrance for ice blockage; clear dead bees from bottom boards if accessible. Verify emergency fondant placed in December is still present. Order supplies: pollen substitute, sugar for syrup, replacement frames with drawn comb, Varroa treatments for the year.

**Week 3 (Feb 15–21): Watch for cleansing flights.** On days when air temperature reaches **10–12°C**, bees will take cleansing flights. In Baia Mare, this typically occurs in late February during warm spells, though some years it delays until early March. After a confirmed cleansing flight, place **protein-energy cakes** (turte proteice: 1 part pollen substitute, 1 part fondant/powdered sugar, bound with honey) directly on top of the cluster — **150–250 g per colony**. Do not give liquid syrup yet; nighttime temperatures are still sub-zero and syrup can chill the cluster.

**Week 4 (Feb 22–28): First colony assessment.** On a calm day above 12°C, perform a quick external assessment. Heft hives to gauge remaining stores — colonies below **8 kg total weight** need immediate fondant. Note which colonies have active pollen foragers (hazel catkins may be opening). Rank colonies as strong, medium, or weak. Identify any dead-outs. Begin planning helper colony assignments: the weakest 2–3 surviving colonies with acceptable queens become helpers; the strongest 4–5 become production colonies.

### Spring awakening and stimulative feeding (Weeks 5–8: March 1–28)

**Week 5 (Mar 1–7): Begin stimulative feeding.** Once daytime temperatures consistently reach **10°C+** and another flight day is expected within 3 days, begin thin syrup feeding: **1:1 sugar-to-water ratio, 200 ml per colony every 3 days**. Add 2 g citric acid per liter for sugar inversion. Feed using top feeders to avoid robbing. The goal is to simulate a light nectar flow, triggering the queen to increase egg-laying. If nights still drop well below freezing, continue with fondant/protein cakes instead of liquid syrup. Replenish pollen patties (150 g) weekly.

**Week 6 (Mar 8–14): First internal inspection.** On a warm day (>14°C), perform the spring summary inspection. Assess: queen presence (eggs visible?), brood pattern quality, colony size in frames of bees, remaining stores, signs of Nosema (dysentery streaks). **Record everything.** Cull any colonies with failing queens or disease. For helper colonies, confirm queen quality — helpers need prolific queens too. Remove any mouse guards that are restricting traffic. Willow catkins may begin opening — the first real pollen and nectar source.

**Week 7 (Mar 15–21): Nest expansion begins.** For colonies covering 5+ frames, **add one drawn comb at the edge of the brood nest** (position 3 or position next-to-last). Continue 1:1 syrup stimulation every 3 days. Willow is now in bloom — monitor pollen loads coming in (grey-yellow = willow). This natural pollen combined with your patties accelerates brood rearing dramatically. Helper colonies receive identical treatment — their job is to build brood as fast as possible.

**Week 8 (Mar 22–28): Accelerate buildup.** Weather permitting (daytime 14°C+), add another frame of drawn comb into the brood nest — now placing it **within the brood area** ("breaking the nest" / spargerea cuibului) if the colony is strong enough (6+ frames of bees). This stimulates the queen to lay across a broader area. Target: production colonies should have **4–5 frames with brood** by end of March. Continue syrup and pollen feeding. Cornelian cherry and early fruit tree buds are swelling.

### April: the critical buildup month (Weeks 9–13)

This is the most consequential month of the year. Every egg laid in April produces a forager for the salcâm flow.

**Week 9 (Mar 29 – Apr 4): Push toward 6 frames of brood.** Insert another drawn comb into the brood nest center for strong colonies. Production colonies should have **5–6 frames with brood** and be covering 7–8 frames of bees. Continue stimulative feeding — increase to **300 ml of 1:1 syrup every 2–3 days** if natural forage is limited. Cherry and plum buds are opening; dandelion rosettes are visible. Helper colonies should also be expanding brood nests aggressively. If any helper colony is lagging, consider supplementing it with a frame of capped brood from a strong production colony — this is the one time production colonies donate to helpers.

**Week 10 (Apr 6–12): Target brood nest maximum.** This is the **critical egg-laying window** — eggs laid this week become foragers around May 18–24, right at salcâm peak. Production colonies should have **7 frames of brood** (or 8–9 for exceptional queens). Helper colonies should be at **6–7 frames of brood**. Dandelion is blooming; cherry trees are in full flower. If you can detect natural nectar income (fresh white wax on comb edges, nectar sheen on cells), you may reduce or stop syrup feeding — but continue pollen patties if natural pollen is insufficient. Begin Varroa monitoring: insert sticky boards for 3-day mite counts. If natural fall exceeds **1 mite/day**, note for post-harvest treatment planning.

**Week 11 (Apr 13–19): First helper colony extractions.** Inspect helpers for frames of **mostly-capped brood** (>75% capped surface). Extract **1 frame of capped brood per helper** colony. Replace with drawn comb. Transfer these frames to production colonies: place each capped brood frame **behind a follower board/dummy frame** or beyond a queen excluder divider within the brood box, so the production queen cannot access it. Emerging bees will join the production colony's workforce. This week's extracted bees will emerge within 1–12 days and become foragers by mid-to-late May. Apple trees are blooming; plum and cherry are at peak or fading.

**Week 12 (Apr 20–26): Second helper extraction + swarm monitoring.** Extract another frame of capped brood from each helper (if they've recovered to 5+ frames of brood). Transfer to production colonies as before. **Begin weekly swarm inspections**: check every production colony for queen cells, especially along frame bottoms and edges. Carniolan genetics make this non-negotiable. If queen cells are found with larvae/royal jelly, remove them. Ensure the brood nest is not congested — if all 10 frames are occupied and bees are bearding, the colony needs vertical space immediately. Apple bloom is at peak; dandelion is peaking; maple species may be flowering.

**Week 13 (Apr 27 – May 3): Add first supers and prepare for salcâm.** Place the **queen excluder and first half-shallow super** on all production colonies. The super should contain drawn comb if available (foundation delays the flow capture by several days). Continue helper extractions (round 3). Production colonies should now have **7+ frames of brood plus 2–3 frames of emerged helper bees** — effective population approaching **50,000+ bees**. Swarm checks are critical this week. If a colony is clearly preparing to swarm despite management, perform a preemptive split: move the old queen and 2 frames of bees/brood to a nuc, letting the production colony raise a new queen (but this costs you the salcâm crop from that colony — prevent rather than cure). Watch night temperatures: salcâm buds are elongating.

### May: the salcâm flow (Weeks 14–17)

**Week 14 (May 4–10): Final pre-flow preparations.** Salcâm buds are 2–3 cm long and developing rapidly. Night temperatures approaching **14°C** signal imminent bloom. Ensure every production colony has at least **1 super on**, preferably 2 if the colony is very strong. Stop all syrup feeding immediately — you do not want sugar syrup in your acacia honey. Final helper colony extraction before the flow — round 4. This is the last extraction before salcâm; let helpers recover during the flow. Total capped brood frames added per production colony over 4 weeks: **3–4 frames, representing ~18,000–24,000 additional bees**.

**Week 15 (May 11–17): Salcâm bloom begins (early-normal season).** In a normal year, black locust flowers open in Baia Mare around **May 15** (±5 days). The pendulous white flower clusters emit a powerful fragrance detectable from hundreds of meters. Bees shift foraging dramatically — returning foragers carry extremely light, water-white nectar. **Do not open hives during peak flow** unless absolutely necessary; every inspection costs approximately 1 kg of lost honey production. Monitor super fill rate from outside: heft the back of the hive daily. If a super gains **2+ kg in a day**, the flow is strong. Add a second super under the first (bottom-supering keeps bees drawing up through already-started combs). If night temperatures drop below 14°C, expect nectar secretion to stall temporarily.

**Week 16 (May 18–24): Peak salcâm flow.** This is the **highest-value week of the entire year**. Strong colonies can bring in **3–5 kg of nectar per day** during peak conditions. Management: add supers as needed (a third super if the first two are >70% full). If you have more drawn comb, use it. Monitor for rain forecasts — heavy rain during salcâm washes nectar from the pendulous flowers and can end the flow overnight. If the flow is exceptional and supers are filling faster than expected, prioritize extracting a full super and returning it rather than running out of space. The colony must never become honey-bound — this triggers swarming and stops the queen from laying (which affects your linden flow workforce 6 weeks later).

**Week 17 (May 25–31): Late salcâm, prepare for transition.** Salcâm flowers are browning and dropping. The flow tapers and ends. **Harvest salcâm honey** within 3–5 days of the flow ending — acacia honey has very low moisture content (typically 16–17%) and is ready quickly, but leaving it risks contamination with the next flow's nectar. Extract all supers, process honey, and return wet supers for bees to clean. The gap period begins.

### June: the gap and early linden (Weeks 18–22)

**Week 18 (Jun 1–7): Post-salcâm gap management.** This 3–4 week gap between salcâm and linden is critical. Without major nectar income, colonies may: (a) consume stores, (b) reduce brood rearing, or (c) prepare to swarm if congested. Resume **light stimulative feeding (1:1 syrup, 300 ml every 3 days)** if no natural nectar is coming in — check combs for fresh nectar; its absence means feeding is needed. Minor sources (Amorpha, raspberry in hill areas, meadow wildflowers, white clover) provide some forage. Resume helper colony extractions: extract 1 frame of capped brood per helper, transfer to production colonies to maintain forager population for linden. Swarm monitoring continues — June is peak swarming season for Carniolan genetics.

**Week 19 (Jun 8–14): Maintain population for linden.** Continue gap management. Catalpa trees in parks and along streets may begin blooming — their large white trumpet flowers are moderately nectariferous. Second round of gap-period helper extractions. Calculate: eggs laid this week become foragers around July 20–26 — this is late for linden but supports late-flow and fall buildup. The priority is maintaining existing foragers, not growing new ones. Ensure production colonies still have **6–7 frames of brood** — if the queen has reduced laying due to the nectar dearth, stimulative feeding is essential.

**Week 20 (Jun 15–21): Early linden scouting.** *Tilia platyphyllos* (large-leaved linden) may begin opening in warm, sheltered locations. Watch for bees returning with dark amber, intensely fragrant nectar — linden honey has an unmistakable sharp, minty-medicinal scent even as fresh nectar. **Place clean supers** on all production colonies (use fresh supers or thoroughly extracted ones to keep linden honey separate from acacia). Last helper extraction before linden flow. Stop stimulative feeding the moment linden flow is confirmed.

**Week 21 (Jun 22–28): Linden flow building.** *Tilia cordata* (small-leaved linden) — the **dominant linden species in Maramureș** — begins blooming. Tiny yellowish-white flowers with wing-like bracts appear on the characteristic long stalks. Nectar secretion is optimal at **16–26°C with high humidity**; warm days following cool, dewy nights are ideal. Add supers as needed. The linden flow is typically less explosive than salcâm but lasts longer (12–16 days for T. cordata). Monitor weather: temperatures above 35°C or below 10°C halt secretion; heavy rain or strong wind destroys flowers.

**Week 22 (Jun 29 – Jul 5): Peak linden flow.** Management mirrors the salcâm peak: minimize inspections, add supers, monitor weight gain. Strong colonies may bring in **2–4 kg/day** in ideal conditions. However, linden is notoriously unreliable — Romanian beekeepers say "even if it smells wonderful, linden often doesn't secrete." Some years produce abundant honey; others yield nothing despite vigorous blooming. **If the flow is strong, manage it aggressively with supers. If weak, accept it and focus on fall preparation.**

### July: harvest and transition (Weeks 23–26)

**Week 23 (Jul 6–12): Late linden, begin harvest planning.** T. cordata flowers are browning. Check supers — capped frames are ready for harvest. Uncapped frames with >80% coverage can be harvested if a refractometer confirms moisture below 18.5%. Plan your extraction schedule.

**Week 24 (Jul 13–19): Harvest linden honey.** Pull all supers. Extract and process linden honey separately from acacia — Romanian monofloral linden honey commands strong prices. Return wet supers briefly for cleaning, then remove and store. **This marks the end of the main honey production season.** Any remaining minor flows (late wildflowers, occasional sunflower if cultivated nearby) are maintenance flows, not production flows.

**Week 25 (Jul 20–26): Begin Varroa treatment immediately.** The moment supers are off, **install amitraz strips** (Varachet Forte, Apitraz, or similar — 2 strips per colony hung between brood frames). Treatment duration: 6–8 weeks (strips remain until early September). Alternatively, use formic acid (MAQS strips or Nassenheider evaporator with 60–65% formic acid) for a shorter treatment window (7–10 days) — formic acid penetrates cappings and kills mites on developing brood, which amitraz strips cannot. **This is the most important Varroa treatment of the year** because it protects the winter bees that will be reared starting in August.

Simultaneously, perform a thorough colony assessment: queen quality, brood pattern, disease signs, remaining stores. Requeen any colony with a failing queen — there is barely enough time for a new queen to mate and begin laying before the winter bee rearing window.

**Week 26 (Jul 27 – Aug 2): Transition to fall management.** Begin **stimulative feeding for winter bee production**: 2:1 sugar syrup (heavy concentration), **500 ml every 2–3 days**. The purpose is dual: building winter stores and stimulating the queen to continue laying through August. Winter bees reared in August–September develop **fat bodies** (vitellogenin reserves) that allow them to survive 4–6 months — they are physiologically different from summer bees and must be raised on adequate nutrition and low Varroa pressure. Resume pollen substitute patties (200 g every 5–7 days) if natural pollen is scarce. Goldenrod may be starting to bloom.

### August: building winter bees (Weeks 27–30)

**Week 27 (Aug 3–9): Heavy feeding and brood monitoring.** Continue 2:1 syrup at 500 ml per feeding, 2–3 times per week. Target: the queen should be maintaining **5–6 frames of brood** through August. This brood will produce the winter bee generation. Helper colonies receive the same treatment — they must also build winter populations. Monitor Varroa strips: if using amitraz, check mite drop on sticky boards. Mite fall should decline over the treatment period.

**Week 28 (Aug 10–16): Assess stores.** Each colony needs **15–20 kg of sealed honey stores** for the Baia Mare winter (5–6 months of cold). Heft hives regularly. A Dadant deep frame full of capped honey holds approximately **3.5–4 kg**. A colony wintering on 7 frames needs roughly 5 full frames of honey surrounding the cluster. If stores are below target, increase feeding volume — you can give **1–2 liters of 2:1 syrup per feeding** at this stage, every 2 days. The deadline is approaching.

**Week 29 (Aug 17–23): Comb management.** Remove any old, dark combs (>3 years old) — dark comb harbors disease spores and pesticide residues. Replace with newer comb or simply tighten the nest. Ensure **pollen stores (bee bread)** are present — at least **0.5 kg** (roughly one full frame of bee bread). If natural pollen income has stopped, continue pollen patties.

**Week 30 (Aug 24–30): Final feeding push.** Romanian practice recommends completing fall feeding by **August 25–September 10**. Push remaining syrup hard this week. Bees need warm temperatures to process and cap syrup — waiting until September risks them unable to dehydrate stores before cold weather. Colonies should be reaching target weight.

### September: closing down (Weeks 31–34)

**Week 31 (Aug 31 – Sep 6): Remove Varroa strips.** If amitraz strips were installed in Week 25, they have now been in place for 6+ weeks — remove them. Do a 3-day mite count with sticky boards. If mite fall still exceeds **1 mite/day**, plan for a follow-up treatment (oxalic acid sublimation during the broodless period in November–December). Complete any remaining feeding — **absolute deadline is September 10–15** in Baia Mare.

**Week 32 (Sep 7–13): Nest tightening (restricting to occupied frames).** Remove any frames not covered by bees. Use follower boards (diaphragms) to restrict the nest to **6–8 frames** — only frames the cluster can cover. This prevents moisture buildup on unused comb and focuses the cluster for winter. Arrange frames: honey frames on the outside, brood/bee bread frames in the center. The cluster will form in the center and eat outward/upward through winter.

**Week 33 (Sep 14–20): Combine weak colonies.** Any colony with fewer than **5 frames of bees** will not survive the Baia Mare winter. Combine weak colonies using the newspaper method: place the weaker colony above the stronger colony separated by a sheet of newspaper with small slits. Bees chew through and unite within 24–48 hours. Kill the inferior queen before combining. This applies to both production and helper colonies — helper colonies that served their purpose can be combined into production colonies to create powerful wintering units.

**Week 34 (Sep 21–27): Install mouse guards.** Reduce all entrances to approximately **8 mm height** using metal entrance reducers — this admits bees but excludes mice. In Maramureș, mice begin seeking shelter in October; guards must be in place before the first cold nights. Verify bottom boards are secure. Check for any gaps or cracks in hive bodies that could admit drafts or mice.

### October: winterizing (Weeks 35–38)

**Week 35 (Sep 28 – Oct 4): Ventilation setup.** Moisture is the **primary winter killer**, not cold. Configure ventilation as follows: if using screened bottom boards (anti-varroa soclu), leave the screen **open** — this eliminates frame mold, the biggest problem in traditional Romanian wintering. Close or reduce top ventilation to prevent heat loss. If using solid bottom boards, ensure the inner cover allows moisture to escape upward through breathable material (burlap, cotton cloth — never plastic or polystyrene directly on frames). Some Romanian beekeepers place **newspaper or absorbent material above the frames** to wick condensation.

**Week 36 (Oct 5–11): Final inspections.** Last full inspection before winter. Confirm: adequate stores (heft test — target **30+ kg total hive weight** for a populated Dadant with stores), queen presence (not strictly necessary to see her — look for recent eggs or very young larvae), healthy brood pattern in the last brood cycle, no signs of American Foulbrood or Nosema. Tilt hives very slightly forward (~2° angle) so any rain or condensation drains out the entrance rather than pooling on the bottom board.

**Week 37 (Oct 12–18): External protection.** If hives are wooden (not polyurethane), wrap in **black polyethylene sheeting or tar paper** for wind protection and solar heat absorption on winter sun days. Leave the entrance unobstructed. Position hives with entrances facing **south or southeast** to capture morning sun and protect from prevailing northwest winds. Place a windbreak (fence, hedge, hay bales) on the north side if the apiary is exposed.

**Week 38 (Oct 19–25): Season complete. Shift to monitoring mode.** The queen stops laying after the first sustained night frosts (typically late October in Baia Mare). Three weeks later, all brood will have emerged — the colony enters its **broodless period**, usually in **mid-to-late November**. Mark this date on your calendar: it is your window for **oxalic acid sublimation** (2.0–2.25 g per colony, applied at **3–13°C** without opening the hive). One sublimation during the true broodless period achieves **>95% mite kill** and sends colonies into winter with minimal Varroa load. This single treatment in November–December is the foundation of next year's colony health.

## The economics of helper colonies: why this system works

The helper colony system is not merely "sharing brood" — it exploits **Farrar's Law**, the non-linear relationship between colony size and honey production. A colony of 60,000 bees produces **1.54× more honey** than a colony of 15,000 bees — not 4× as simple arithmetic would suggest, but dramatically more per bee. By concentrating bees from helper colonies into fewer production colonies, you push each production colony above the population threshold where foraging efficiency spikes. Four production colonies boosted to 70,000+ bees each will out-produce seven average colonies of 40,000 bees.

The helper colonies sacrifice their own honey crop but serve three additional functions beyond brood donation: they act as **swarm-pressure relief valves** (removing brood from congested production colonies), they provide **insurance queens** (if a production colony goes queenless, a helper queen can be transferred), and they can be **recombined in fall** to create powerful wintering units. A hobbyist running this system effectively operates a 7–8 colony apiary while harvesting as if running 6–7 maxed-out production colonies.

## Varroa management integrated calendar

The annual Varroa strategy for Baia Mare follows three treatment windows:

- **Post-harvest (late July)**: Amitraz strips for 6–8 weeks, or formic acid for 7–10 days. Protects winter bee rearing. This is mandatory.
- **Broodless period (November–December)**: Oxalic acid sublimation, single application, >95% efficacy. This is mandatory.
- **Spring (March–April)**: Only if autumn treatment was inadequate and natural mite fall exceeds 1 mite/day. Use oxalic acid glycerin strips or drone brood trapping. Most years, proper fall treatment makes spring treatment unnecessary.

**Rotate chemistry annually** — alternate amitraz years with formic acid years to prevent resistance buildup. Always purchase treatments from physical veterinary pharmacies (online sale of veterinary medicines is prohibited in Romania by law).

### How to use the bloom calendar as a real-time season tracker

Phenological events are the most reliable predictor of upcoming flows. Monitor these sequential bloom markers and compare against the "normal" dates above:

- **If willow blooms before March 10** → season is 7+ days early → expect salcâm around May 8–12
- **If cherry blooms April 10–15** → season is normal → expect salcâm around May 15–22
- **If cherry hasn't bloomed by April 22** → season is late → expect salcâm May 25–June 3
- **Black locust buds elongating to 3 cm** → bloom in ~2 weeks
- **Apple petals falling** → salcâm is approximately 2–3 weeks away
- **Salcâm flowers browning** → linden T. cordata begins in 20–30 days

Record your own observations each year to build a site-specific phenology database. After 3–4 seasons, your personal bloom dates will be more accurate than any published calendar.

## Conclusion: precision timing over brute force

The single most valuable insight for this apiary system is that **colony population is not a snapshot but a pipeline**. The foragers working the salcâm flow in late May were eggs in early April — decisions made 6 weeks before the flow determines its harvest. The helper colony system amplifies this pipeline by adding an external supply of emerging bees precisely when the production colony's own brood-to-forager conversion is at capacity. Seven frames of brood plus 3–4 frames of donated capped brood per production colony creates a temporary population **25–35% above natural equilibrium**, concentrating this surplus into the narrow 8–12 day salcâm window where every additional forager translates directly into harvestable honey. The same logic applies to linden, though the longer bloom window and lower flow intensity make the timing less knife-edge. Combined with aggressive post-harvest Varroa treatment to protect August's winter bees and proper winterizing for Maramureș's 5-month cold season, this system can consistently outperform conventional single-colony management by a significant margin — turning a 5-hive hobbyist apiary into a production-optimized operation.
